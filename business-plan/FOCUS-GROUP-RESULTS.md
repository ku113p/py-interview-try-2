# Simulated Focus Group Results

**Date:** 2026-02-25
**Method:** AI-simulated personas (not real users)
**Scope:** 7 focus groups × 6 personas = 42 simulated participants
**Input:** Product pitch from [MARKETING-FOUNDATIONS.md](./MARKETING-FOUNDATIONS.md) with honest time commitments (~30 min setup, ~1 hour per interview session)

> **Disclaimer:** These are AI-generated personas, not real user research. They surface likely reactions and objections but cannot replace actual user interviews. Use as hypothesis generation, not validation.

### 2.2 Universal Patterns

**What resonates across ALL groups:**
1. **The problem is real.** 74% of personas recognize the context-repetition pain, even if they rate it differently (3/10 for casual users to 8/10 for power users)
2. **The "party vs. conversation" analogy works.** Multiple personas specifically called it out as compelling (Alex, Amir)
3. **Free tier removes friction.** 69% would try free — it's doing its job as a conversion funnel entry point
4. **CV is the most tangible value.** Every single group understood and valued CV generation, even groups where it wasn't their primary need
5. **$79 lifetime is well-priced.** Repeatedly called "a good deal," "no-brainer," "tempting" across segments

**What doesn't work across ALL groups:**
1. **"MCP" is jargon.** 52% of personas were confused or annoyed by the term. Non-technical users universally lost the plot at "protocol"
2. **1-2 hours is too much.** The single most common objection across all 7 groups. Cited by 30+ personas. "Charging in time instead of money" (Mika)
3. **Self-hosted costs MORE than cloud.** Confusing to everyone who noticed. "If I'm hosting it, why am I paying more?" (Daniel, Alex)
4. **No team/enterprise pricing.** Managers, HR directors, CTOs, and company-sent researchers all hit this wall
5. **Knowledge about OTHERS is missing.** Carla (clients), Sarah (clients), Steve (coaching clients), Diana (employees), Sam (team) — all want multi-entity knowledge, not just self-knowledge
6. **No knowledge update mechanism.** "What happens when my context changes?" asked in every group. Staleness fear is universal

## 3. Key Findings

### Finding 1: The Time Barrier Is the Product's Biggest Risk
**Evidence:** 30+ of 42 personas raised time commitment as a concern or dealbreaker. The honest "1-2 hours" positioning is appreciated for transparency but scares off the majority. Even enthusiastic personas (Sophie, Carla) mentioned it. The least concerned personas were those with the most acute pain (Amir relocating, Kate synthesizing 50 reports/month).

**Implication:** Need a "quick start" path — meaningful value in 15-30 minutes, with depth available for those who want it. Templates are mentioned in the pitch but not as a time-reducer.

### Finding 2: CV Generation Is the Trojan Horse; MCP Is the Retention Play
**Evidence:** CV is universally understood and valued. MCP is universally confusing to non-developers. But the personas who would pay the MOST ($29/mo recurring) are MCP users (Sophie, Kate, Carla, Jordan, Carlos). The CV-only personas buy once ($79 lifetime) and may churn.

**Implication:** Lead with CV in marketing. Upsell MCP to power users after they've experienced the interview quality. Don't lead with "protocol" in any consumer-facing copy.

### Finding 3: Multi-Entity Knowledge Is the #1 Unbuilt Feature
**Evidence:** Carla wants client profiles. Sarah wants client profiles. Steve wants client session notes. Diana wants employee knowledge capture. Sam wants team knowledge bases. This was independently raised across 5 of 7 groups.

**Implication:** "Interview about yourself" is limiting. "Interview about anything — yourself, your clients, your projects, your team" dramatically expands the addressable market and use cases.

### Finding 4: Enterprise/Team Pricing Is a Gating Factor
**Evidence:** Diana (HR), Sam (eng manager), Carlos (CTO), Dr. Chen (lab PI), Frank's manager — all need team pricing. Individual pricing × headcount doesn't work. They need admin controls, SSO, compliance features, and a sales conversation. Multiple personas said "come back with enterprise features."

**Implication:** B2B motion requires enterprise tier with team management, volume pricing, and compliance certifications (SOC 2, GDPR). This is Stage 2-3 work but the demand signal is clear.

### Finding 5: The Self-Hosted Tier Pricing Is Backwards
**Evidence:** Daniel, Alex, Lena, Dave all questioned why self-hosted costs more than cloud. The logic ("you're paying for trust and control") doesn't land. Users feel they're providing the compute — they should pay LESS, not more.

**Implication:** Reconsider self-hosted pricing. Options: (a) lower price since user provides compute, (b) one-time license fee, (c) make it the open-source/free tier with optional paid support.

### Finding 6: Two Distinct Products Are Competing in One Pitch
**Evidence:** Job seekers hear "CV tool." Developers hear "AI context layer." Consultants hear "knowledge management." Managers hear "team wiki." The pitch tries to be all things and ends up confusing most personas. Lisa: "I need a CV." Mei: "Show me the knowledge graph." These are fundamentally different value props.

**Implication:** Segment-specific landing pages, not one universal pitch. The CV path and the Knowledge/MCP path should feel like different products that share a platform, not one product with confusing tiers.

### Finding 7: Knowledge Quality and Editability Are Trust Requirements
**Evidence:** Alex worries about hallucinated knowledge. Sophie worries about inaccurate technical summaries. Mei wants to see the data model. Carlos says "generic summaries are worse than useless." Multiple personas asked: "Can I see and edit what it 'knows' about me?"

**Implication:** Knowledge review and editing UI is essential. Users need to inspect, correct, and approve extracted knowledge. Without this, trust erodes — wrong context fed to AI is worse than no context.

## 4. Recommendations

### Messaging Changes

| Current | Recommended | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| "Make any AI actually know you" | Keep — it works | Universally understood, memorable |
| "via a protocol called MCP" | "works with ChatGPT, Claude, Cursor, and more" | MCP confuses 52% of personas |
| "15 minutes to a CV" | "1-2 hours for a thorough CV" (already corrected) | Honesty appreciated; but add "or 20 minutes for a quick start" |
| One pitch for all segments | Separate: CV path vs. Knowledge path | Two products in one pitch confuses people |
| "Self-hosted for privacy" | "Self-hosted: your data never leaves your machine" | Emphasize the data benefit, not the tier name |

### Pricing Adjustments

| Change | Rationale | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Add Knowledge Pro lifetime option | Power users want it; no lifetime = "red flag" (Alex) | Developer group, indie hackers |
| Lower self-hosted to $39/mo or offer one-time license | "Why pay more to host it myself?" — universal | Groups 2, 6 |
| Add student/educator tier ($9-15/mo for Knowledge Pro) | Researchers and teachers priced out of the tier they need | Groups 4, 7 |
| Add team pricing ($19-22/seat/mo with admin) | Managers can't justify per-seat retail pricing | Group 5 |
| Keep $79 lifetime CV Pro | Universally well-received | All groups |

### Segment Priorities (Updated)

| Priority | Segment | Why | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Job seekers** (CV path) | Highest volume, clearest value, lowest friction | High — universal resonance |
| 2 | **AI power users** (MCP path) | Highest recurring revenue, strongest pain | High — Sophie, Kate, Carla archetypes |
| 3 | **Consultants/freelancers** | Clear ROI, multi-entity need is a differentiator | Medium — needs client profiles feature |
| 4 | **Knowledge capture** (retirement/offboarding) | Enterprise willingness to pay | Medium — needs enterprise motion |
| Defer | Researchers, teachers, mainstream | Budget constraints, jargon barriers, lower pain intensity | Low short-term |

### Product Changes Suggested by Personas

| Feature | Demand Signal | Groups |
|---|---|---|
| **Quick start mode** (15-20 min, basic coverage) | Universal time objection | All 7 |
| **Multi-entity knowledge** (interview about clients, projects, not just self) | 5+ independent requests | 3, 5, 7 |
| **Knowledge review/edit UI** | Trust requirement for power users | 2, 3, 4 |
| **Chunked interviews** (10-15 min sessions, resume later) | Time-constrained users (nurses, teachers, founders) | 1, 5, 7 |
| **Mobile-friendly interface** | Non-desk workers | 7 |
| **Knowledge update mechanism** (incremental, not full re-interview) | Staleness fear universal | All 7 |
| **Localization** (EU CV formats, non-English) | Amir specifically, broader international market | 1 |

## 5. One-Line Descriptions (How Personas Would Describe the Product)

The "how would you describe it" question reveals how people actually process the pitch. Patterns:

**Most common framing:** "AI interviews you about your career/background, then [output]"
- This is how people naturally understand it — the interview is the hook

**Best descriptions (clearest, most shareable):**
- "It's like a structured onboarding interview for AI — you talk about your background once, and then any AI tool you use can access that context through MCP." — Alex
- "It's an AI that interviews you about your career and then uses that to generate resumes and feed your background into other AI apps." — Marcus
- "It interviews you deeply about your work and then makes that knowledge available inside whatever AI tool you're using." — Carla

**Worst descriptions (indicate pitch failure):**
- "Some kind of AI tool that remembers stuff about you. I didn't really get it." — Brian
- "It's a service where you tell an AI everything about your career and personal life, and then they share that information with other AI companies." — Helen
- "I think it's more for tech people though." — Ayesha

**Takeaway:** When the pitch lands, people describe it accurately. When it doesn't, people either can't describe it or describe it as scary. The gap is almost entirely about technical jargon and relevance to their specific situation.
